# Your Events Director

## Who They Are

Your Events Director is your event planning powerhouse. They're high-energy, deadline-obsessed, and always working backwards from the event date. They handle everything from big-picture event strategy to the minute-by-minute run of show.

They know that nonprofit events serve multiple goals at once -- fundraising, community building, donor stewardship, awareness -- and they design with all of that in mind.

---

## What They're Great At

- Building comprehensive event plans with timelines and budgets
- Writing minute-by-minute run of show documents
- Designing tiered sponsorship packages and prospectuses
- Writing sponsorship outreach emails and follow-ups
- Building post-event evaluation reports and ROI analysis
- Coordinating vendor lists and staffing plans
- Designing attendee communications (invites, reminders, thank-yous)
- Planning galas, 5Ks, community events, and virtual events

---

## When to Go to Them

Go to your Events Director when you need to:

- Plan an event from scratch and don't know where to start
- Build a sponsorship program for an upcoming fundraiser
- Write a run of show for a gala or community event
- Evaluate how your last event did financially and operationally
- Figure out how to make your annual gala more profitable
- Draft outreach to potential sponsors
- Build a checklist of everything that needs to happen before, during, and after an event

---

## Example Prompts to Try

Copy and paste any of these to get started:

1. "Help me plan our annual gala. Date: [date]. Venue: [venue]. Expected guests: [number]. Revenue goal: [amount]."
2. "Build a run of show for a 3-hour fundraising dinner. Start time: 6pm. Key moments: cocktails, dinner, auction, program."
3. "Design a tiered sponsorship package for our 5K. Revenue goal: $20,000."
4. "Write a sponsorship pitch email for local businesses."
5. "We just finished our gala. Help me write a post-event report for the board."
6. "Build a 90-day event timeline for our fall fundraiser."
7. "Draft an invitation email for our spring luncheon. Audience: major donors."
8. "What vendors do I need for a 200-person outdoor community event?"
9. "Help me design a volunteer staffing plan for our walk/run event."
10. "Calculate the ROI of our last event. Revenue: [X]. Expenses: [Y]. Hours donated: [Z]."

> **Try this now:** "I'm planning a [type of event] for [date]. Help me build a project checklist with everything I need to do in the next 90 days."

---

## Tips for Working With Them

**Give them your constraints first.** Budget, venue size, guest count, and date are the four numbers that drive everything. Share these upfront and they'll plan within them.

**Use them for run of show.** This is where they shine -- the detailed, time-stamped document that keeps your whole team on the same page day-of.

**Share past event data.** If you have revenue, attendance, and expense data from past events, paste it in. They'll use it to help you improve.

**Vendors and venues are local decisions.** They can give you a framework and a vendor checklist, but specific vendor recommendations depend on your community. You'll know who to call.
