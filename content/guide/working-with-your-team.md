# Working With Your Team

## How to Give Good Instructions

Your AI team is capable -- but they need clear instructions to deliver their best work. Think of it the same way you'd think about delegating to a new staff member: the clearer your brief, the better the output.

### The Four Things That Always Help

**1. Say what you need (the task)**
Be specific. "Write a grant proposal" is vague. "Write a 500-word program narrative for the Ford Foundation grant, focused on our youth mentoring program" is a real brief.

**2. Give context**
Who is the audience? What's the deadline? Is this internal or external? Does it need to match a specific tone?

**3. Share the raw material**
Paste in the funder guidelines, the email thread you're responding to, the data you want summarized. Your team can't work from what's in your head.

**4. Tell them what "done" looks like**
Should this be a bullet list or a full draft? Should it be ready to send or ready to edit? Is this a first pass or a final version?

---

## How to Review Outputs

Your team produces solid first drafts. Here's how to make them better:

**Read it like your audience would.** Don't just check for errors -- ask whether someone who doesn't know your org would understand it.

**Catch what only you know.** Your team doesn't know the specific person you're writing to, the local context, or the internal politics. Those details are yours to add.

**Use tracked changes or comments.** If you're editing a long draft, add your notes directly. Then paste the marked-up version back in and ask for a revision.

**Be direct about what's wrong.** "This doesn't sound like us" is feedback. "This is too formal -- we speak conversationally and we lead with community voice" is better feedback that produces a better revision.

---

## How to Request Revisions

Don't throw away a draft because it's not quite right. Revising is faster than starting over.

**Tell them what to change, not just that you don't like it.**
Bad: "This isn't quite right."
Good: "The tone is too formal. Shorten the second paragraph. Add a call to action at the end."

**Paste the section you want changed.** Point to specific sentences and say what should be different.

**Ask for multiple options.** "Give me 3 versions of this opening paragraph, each with a different approach" is a great use of your team.

---

## How to Get Better Over Time

Your team gets smarter as your organization's memory grows. A few things you can do:

**Add to your Memory.** The Memory section of your dashboard is where your team stores what they know about your org. The more you add, the more personalized their output becomes.

**Be consistent with feedback.** If you always come back with the same note ("we never say 'synergy'"), consider adding that to your brand voice guidelines in Memory.

**Work with multiple team members on complex decisions.** A fundraising strategy might benefit from your Development Director's revenue lens AND your Marketing Director's messaging expertise. Use both.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Starting too broad.** "Help me with communications" is too vague. Start specific and expand.
- **Not sharing your audience.** Always say who will read or hear this.
- **Expecting perfection on the first try.** Iteration is the process. Budget one or two rounds of revision on any serious piece.
- **Not reviewing before sending.** Your team is fast and capable, but you're the final check on everything that goes out with your name on it.
